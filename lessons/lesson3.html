Lesson 3: Photoresists and Their Applications

Photoresist Application

Photoresist Types
There are two main types of photoresists: positive and negative. Positive photoresists become more soluble upon exposure to light, while negative photoresists become less soluble. Step 1: Spin-Coating
The wafer is placed on a spin coater, and the photoresist material is applied to the wafer surface. The wafer spins rapidly, spreading the photoresist evenly. Step 2: Soft Bake
The wafer is heated to remove any remaining solvent and pre-harden the photoresist. Step 3: Exposing
The wafer is exposed to UV light through a photomask, transferring the pattern onto the photoresist. Step 4: Post-Exposure Bake
The wafer is heated again to stabilize the pattern in the photoresist. Step 5: Developing
The wafer is immersed in a developer solution, which selectively removes either the exposed or unexposed parts of the photoresist, revealing the desired pattern. Step 6: Hard Bake
A final baking step is performed to further harden the photoresist and make it resistant to etching processes. Applications
Photoresists are crucial in various applications such as semiconductor fabrication, microelectronics, and nanotechnology. Note: The animation shown here is a simplified representation for educational purposes.
